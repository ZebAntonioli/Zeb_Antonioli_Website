Introduction

Panthera leo are the largest terrestrial predators in Africa (Bauer et al., 2015). Coined the “King of the Jungle”, most know them to be ferocious powerful predators to be feared however, my experience both in Botswana and Kenya made me realize that lions often behave like over-sized house cats that could still kill you if they wanted to. From sunbathing, big stretches, and lots of naps, they were a joy to observe and photograph. There may also be something off with me as one particular evening in the Kalahari of Botswana I got to be up close and personal with a large male. I was alone in my vehicle with the guide and other cars started to pull up to get a look. This particular male was one of two brothers that were the leaders of one of the prides in the region. In the distance you could hear his brother roaring, and he replied. There is something incredible about feeling a lions roar reverberating through your body. While I heard other tourists start to quickly ask their guides to get them out of there, my guide Spike turned to me, saw me smiling like a fool, and kept the vehicle right there. It is a moment I will never forget.

Individual variation within parasite communities of endangered African lions. Harris & Hallam, 2025

Harris and Hallam collected carnivore fecal samples from Senegal, Benin, and Burkina Faso. The fecal samples were then used for DNA extraction and species determination via cytochrome b to move confirmed lion specific samples. The lion samples were then used for metabarcoding to determine the haemoparasite, nemabiome, and bacterial communities found in the lion feces. The most important finding was the presence of Sacocystis and Ancylostoma which can cause death in certain cases. These parasites combined with the added pressure from human encroachment and conflict are major concerns for the conservation of West African Lions. The parasite differences between populations with added human pressures is still not well understood; however, habitat fragmentation and anthropogenic pressures have been shown to decrease overall parasite diversity especially those with more complex life-cycles while increasing the carriage of others (Lafferty, 2012). Understanding this in the context of African lions is important for proper conservation efforts and monitoring.

The Gut Microbiome Profile of Lions in Etosha National Park, Namibia. Belger et al., 2026

This research is currently available as a preprint. The study focused on lions within Etosha National Park in Namibia and performed shotgun metagenomics on the extracted feces. Both long and short read sequencing was done to assemble MAGs of the the bacterial species within the feces. This allowed for both an in depth functional analysis of the bacterial community and characterization of the bacterial community itself. This study is important because it focuses on wild lions rather than captive and produced both a very in depth analysis of the bacterial community structure and the functional pathways being used by the bacterial occupants. They found that not only were there multiple novel species making up variable percentages of the bacterial community, but they also found enriched purine metabolism. The characterization of these novel microbiome community members shows that additional research needs to be conducted to determine who these mystery members are.
