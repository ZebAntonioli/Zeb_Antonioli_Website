Introduction

Bison bison are a symbol of the western United States. Before westward expansion, herds would take days to pass through valleys (Epp & Dyck, 2002). Now after the near extermination of them, there are some scattered herds in various National Parks, Monuments, and private conservation areas (Sanderson et al., 2008). Growing up in Montana only a few hours away from Yellowstone National Park, I became quite acquainted with these charismatic creatures. From being stuck in Bison Jams to having groups pass alarmingly close when set up on the side of the road, I have had many amazing encounters with these animals. While many tourists want to go pet the fluffy cows, I like to keep my distance, but this has not stopped me from not keeping track of my surroundings while shooting and all of a sudden here passes a cow bison 20 feet from me just plodding along. Thankfully I haven’t been yelled at by any angry park rangers yet, but there is always next time.

Subspecific variation in gut microbiota of North American bison in a sympatric setting reveals differentially abundant taxa. Grant et al., 2025.

Grant et al., investigated the gut microbiota of the two North American subspecies of Bison bison, Bison bison bison (plains bison) and Bison bison athabascae (wood bison). Fecal samples were collected from herds of both subspecies in Elk Island National Park Alberta, Canada. The feces was extracted and using short tandem repeat genotyping individual bison were identified to avoid replicates of the same individual. Subsequent metabarcoding via PCR amplification and sequencing of 16S amplicons was performed to determine the fecal microbiome composition. There were some interesting findings including significantly different alpha diversity metrics when comparing sex and species of the bison indicating some potential species and sexual differences in the overall richness and evenness of the fecal microbiome; however, the overall community similarities were still very close especially when factoring in sex. When comparing the two subspecies via Bray-Curtis Dissimilarity, it was found that they differed significantly; however, the authors noted that this could have been due to the subspecies dispersion in the area rather than true biological differences. Overall, it was found that while there were differences observed between the two subspecies, these differences are potentially due to sample collection bias and not due to biological differences.

Seasonal Shifts in Diet and Gut Microbiota of the American Bison (Bison bison). Bergmann et al., 2015.

Bergmann et al., investigated how seasons shifts in feeding behavior affected the bacterial microbiome of plains bison. Feces was collected from April until September from the bison herd found on the Konza Prairie Biological Station in Kansas. PCR was performed to amplify both diet content and bacterial microbiome via trnL and 16S primers respectively. These amplicons were sequenced and analyzed to determine what shifts occurred over the time span in both the diet and microbiome. What was found is that diet shifts drastically over the course of the season while the microbiome remains fairly static with small changes in specific taxa that are potentially due to diet changes, but the exact relationship remains uncertain. This study incorporates the importance of understanding the temporal changes in the gut microbiome as it is a living community that is subject to change over time especially as diet items change over the course of the seasons.
